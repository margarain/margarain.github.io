About Vigan City

Vigan is a well-preserved example of a planned Spanish colonial town in Asia, and it offers a unique glimpse into the past. It is best known for its cobblestone streets, colonial-era architecture, and charming ambiance. It was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999 and remains one of the most picturesque cities in the Philippines.

More on Calle Crisologo

Calle Crisologo is the heart of Vigan's heritage. The street is closed to vehicle traffic, making it perfect for a leisurely stroll or a ride on a horse-drawn carriage (kalesa). The ground floors of the ancestral houses have been converted into souvenir shops, cafes, and museums. The best time to visit is late in the afternoon and into the evening when the warm glow of the streetlights gives the cobblestones a magical atmosphere.

More on Vigan Sunsets

Vigan offers breathtaking sunsets. One of the best viewing spots is along the Mestizo River via the Vigan River Cruise, which provides a tranquil experience as the sun dips below the horizon. Alternatively, you can head to nearby Mindoro Beach to watch the sunset over the West Philippine Sea, creating a spectacular display of colors.

More on St. Paul Cathedral

The St. Paul Metropolitan Cathedral is a prime example of the "Earthquake Baroque" style, featuring massive buttresses to protect it from natural disasters. Originally built in 1574, it has been reconstructed several times. Its impressive facade and octagonal bell tower, which stands separately from the main church, are iconic landmarks of Vigan's Plaza Salcedo.

More on Palacio Real

The Palacio de Arzobispado, or Archbishop's Palace, is another historical gem. It served as the residence of the archbishop and witnessed significant events in Philippine history, including the stay of revolutionary leader Diego Silang. Today, it houses a museum with a collection of religious artifacts, paintings, and ecclesiastical treasures that tell the story of the Catholic Church in the Ilocos region.

More on Vigan Empanada

The Vigan Empanada is a must-try delicacy. Its distinctive orange-hued, crispy shell is made from rice flour. Inside, you'll find a savory filling of Vigan longganisa, green papaya, and a whole egg. It's freshly fried upon ordering and best enjoyed with a side of sukang Iloko (local vinegar) with chopped onions and chili. You can find stalls selling them all over the city, especially in Plaza Burgos.

More on Bagnet

Bagnet is the Ilocano version of chicharon or lechon kawali. What makes it unique is the meticulous three-stage cooking process: the pork belly is boiled, then air-dried, then deep-fried twice to achieve its signature ultra-crispy skin and succulent, flavorful meat. It's a decadent dish often served with KBL (Kamatis, Bagoong, Lasona - Tomato, Shrimp Paste, Shallots) as a dipping sauce.

More on Pinakbet

The Ilocano Pinakbet is the authentic version of this popular Filipino vegetable stew. Unlike other regional variations, it traditionally uses only vegetables grown in the Ilocos region, such as bitter melon, eggplant, okra, and string beans. The key ingredient that gives it its distinct flavor is "bagoong isda" (fermented fish sauce), which provides a salty, umami depth to the dish.
